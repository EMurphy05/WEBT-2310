Underground Chattanooga

After the devastating 1867 floods, Chattanooga, Tennessee, raised much of its downtown by several feet to prevent future disasters. This bold project created what’s now known as Underground Chattanooga—the original street level, left buried beneath the modern city. Today, this hidden layer stands as a fascinating reminder of Chattanooga’s resilience and ingenuity in turning tragedy into transformation.

The Trail of Tears

Ross’s Landing in Chattanooga was once a thriving Cherokee riverfront community, but in 1838 it became one of the major departure points for the forced removal of the Cherokee people during the Trail of Tears. Thousands were gathered here before being sent west on difficult and often deadly journeys. Today, The Passage exhibit at Ross’s Landing serves as a memorial, using flowing water, carved stone, and interpretive markers to honor Cherokee culture and to remind visitors of the resilience of the people who endured this tragic chapter in history.

Chickamauga National Military Park

The first and largest national military park was established in 1895. Three years later, would briefly host a major training center for troops during the Spanish-American War – Camp Thomas. On the northern edge of the park lies Fort Ogelthorpe – established as a base for the 6th Cavalry in 1902, and housed German prisoners during World War I. During World War II, the fort hosted the third WAC Training Center, one of the largest training sites for women soldiers. Few traces remain today of these facilities as they were returned to the National Park Service in 1946.
